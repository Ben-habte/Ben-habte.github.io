# 1. Introduction

## Problem

Over the past decade, healthcare has become increasingly digitized. From online appointment booking to digital prescriptions and patient portals, technology has transformed how we interact with medical systems. Yet for people living with chronic conditions, daily health management still relies heavily on memory, scattered tools, and manual tracking.

Remembering medications, coordinating appointments, monitoring symptoms, and communicating with providers often happens across multiple apps, paper notes, pill organizers, and phone alarms. As life becomes busier and routines more unpredictable, managing health can begin to feel like a second full-time job.

## Understanding the Problem

Managing a chronic condition requires consistent daily action — taking medications, tracking symptoms, booking appointments, and communicating with providers. Yet these tasks are often spread across multiple tools like phone alarms, paper notes, pill organizers, and separate hospital portals.

This fragmentation increases mental load and makes it easy to forget important steps. Young adults struggle with inconsistent routines, older adults face accessibility barriers, and caregivers experience stress without clear visibility into a loved one’s adherence.

Missed doses and delayed communication can lead to anxiety, worsening symptoms, and preventable complications. The core issue is not simply forgetfulness — it is the lack of flexible, engaging, and accessible tools designed around real-life behaviours.

## Our Mission

At **Health Companion**, our mission is to simplify chronic health management through thoughtful, human-centered design.

We aim to create a **super app** that brings medication reminders, adherence tracking, appointment scheduling, and provider communication into one accessible, calming platform.

By prioritizing **flexibility, clarity, and accessibility**, the Health Companion Super App empowers patients and caregivers to feel organized, supported, and in control of their health — without feeling overwhelmed.

# 2. User Research

## Research Method

**Online Survey (10 participants)**

**Purpose:**  
Understand habits and pain points quickly.

**Types of questions asked:**

- How many medications do you take daily?
- What tools do you use now (alarm, paper, app)?
- What’s your biggest challenge?
- Have you missed medication in the past month?
- Do you struggle with refills or appointment scheduling?

## Key Findings

From the 10 participants:

- **60%** are aged **25 or under**, highlighting that chronic health management is a significant issue for young adults (not only seniors).
- **70%** miss medication **at least weekly**.
- **30%** miss medication **3+ times/week**.
- **20%** of respondents were **caregivers managing someone else's health**.
- The **average medications per day** among patients is **2.4**.

**Table 1:** Raw data (Name replaced to “Person” for privacy)

**Figure 1:** Bar chart illustrating frequency of medication missed.

The majority miss medication weekly or more. This is a strong validation signal that the problem is real.

**Figure 2:** Pie chart illustrating age distribution across three demographic categories.

Most participants are **25 or under**, confirming that medication inconsistency is not only a senior issue but also a young adult behavioral problem.

## Most Common Challenges

- Inconsistent routines
- App engagement
- Forgetfulness / uncertainty
- Accessibility barriers

## Top Requested Features

- Simple, customizable reminders (7/10)
- Appointment scheduling (6/10)
- Clean modern UI with gentle notifications (5/10)
- Caregiver access / monitoring (4/10)
- Refill reminders (4/10)
- Symptom tracking

# 3. Personas

## Persona 1: Maya

Maya is a **23-year-old marketing assistant and part-time student living in Toronto**. She has **ADHD, anxiety, and mild asthma**, and balances work with classes.

Despite being tech-savvy and regularly using digital tools like TikTok and Notion, Maya struggles with maintaining consistent routines — especially when it comes to taking medications.

She is looking for an app that is:

- Simple
- Engaging
- Non-clinical feeling

Her goal is to stay consistent with her health without feeling overwhelmed.

---

## Persona 2: Jay

Jay is a **20-year-old engineering student** who lives with his **74-year-old grandmother, Lola**.

He acts as her **primary caregiver**, managing medications for **Type 2 Diabetes and early-stage dementia**.

Balancing university and caregiving leaves him feeling overwhelmed.

Jay wants:

- A way to **remotely monitor medication adherence**
- Easier **appointment management**
- Less mental load while caring for his grandmother.

# 5. Requirements & Problem Statements

## Primary Problem Statement (Patient Focus)

“Young adults managing chronic conditions with inconsistent routines need a **flexible and engaging way to track medications** because rigid reminders and clinical apps lead to missed doses, reduced focus at work, and increased anxiety.”

## Secondary Problem Statement (Caregiver Focus)

“Gen Z and millennial caregivers managing medication for aging parents need **remote monitoring tools and quick communication with providers** because missed doses and slow clinic response times cause constant stress.”

---

## User Requirements

### For Patients

- Flexible reminders
- Quick 1-tap confirmation
- Engaging visual feedback
- Gentle notifications
- Symptom + medication pairing
- Refill alerts

### For Caregivers

- Caregiver mode
- Missed dose alerts
- Remote confirmation
- Simplified patient view
- 1-tap caregiver call
- Appointment booking + calendar sync
- Provider messaging

### For General Users

- Simple medication setup
- Appointment scheduling
- Secure messaging
- Readable interface

# 6. User Flow Chart

## Flow 1: Add Medication

1. User starts at home screen  
2. Taps **Add Medication**  
3. Enters medication details  
4. Sets reminder time  
5. Confirms  
6. Receives success message  
7. Returns to home screen with medication listed

## Flow 2: Receive Reminder & Confirm Taken

Two possible paths:

**Option 1: TAKEN**

- User taps large **green “TAKEN” button**
- Dose logged
- Caregiver notified

**Option 2: Skip**

- User taps **Skip**
- App prompts for reason
- Data used to understand adherence patterns

# 11. Usability Testing Plan

## Testing Goals

Validate whether users can:

- Add medication independently
- Confirm medication quickly
- Book appointments without confusion
- Navigate easily across screens

Accessibility testing is particularly important for **older adults like Lola**.

---

## Participants

Testing includes **5–8 participants**, ideally:

- 2 older adults (60+)
- 1 caregiver
- 2 general users (young to middle-aged adults)

This ensures representation of **both primary personas**.

---

## Testing Tasks

Participants complete five tasks:

1. Add a medication with reminder at **8:00 AM**
2. Mark medication **taken**
3. Locate **next appointment**
4. **Book an appointment**
5. **Send message to provider**

---

## Success Metrics

- **Task completion rate**
- **Time on task**
- **Number of errors**
- **User satisfaction rating (1–5)**

Benchmarks include:

- Medication setup < 60 seconds
- Confirm medication < 5 seconds

---

## Analysis & Iteration

Results will be analyzed by:

- Identifying confusion patterns
- Prioritizing issues by severity
- Revising wireframes and prototype
- Running a second round of testing

# 12. Feedback Summary + Iterations

## Testing Overview

Testing was conducted with **6 participants**:

- 2 older adults (68, 72)
- 1 caregiver (20)
- 3 general users (23, 24, 45)

---

## Positive Feedback

Participants described the app as:

- “Clean and approachable”
- “Less clinical than other health apps”

Older adults especially liked the **large “TAKEN” button**.

Gentle notifications were also appreciated.

---

## Constructive Feedback

Main issues discovered:

- Medication setup had **too many steps**
- Home screen felt **crowded**
- Appointment confirmation was unclear
- Users wanted **progress motivation (streaks)**

---

## Changes Made

Based on testing, the design was updated:

- Medication setup reduced to **3 steps**
- Simplified home screen
- Added **appointment confirmation screen**
- Introduced **Weekly Streak**
- Increased base text to **16pt**

---

## Second Round Testing

Three participants tested the revised design:

- 1 older adult
- 1 caregiver
- 1 general user

Results:

- Medication setup completed in **under 45 seconds**
- Appointment booking confusion removed
- Streak feature increased motivation

# 13. Reflection

## What UX Helped Me Discover

- Users value **simplicity over advanced features**
- **Accessibility must be built in from the start**
- Caregiver features greatly increase usefulness

## Challenges

- Balancing super-app features without overwhelm
- Designing for both **patients and caregivers**

## How I Overcame Them

- Progressive disclosure
- Separate caregiver mode
- Prioritizing frequent tasks

---

# Final Thoughts

Designing **Health Companion** reinforced a simple truth:

When people are already carrying the weight of their health, the product should **not add to that burden**.

The real challenge was not adding more features — it was **deciding what truly matters and removing the rest**.

Research and testing revealed that **clarity, accessibility, and emotional reassurance** are just as important as functionality.

A **large “TAKEN” button**, a **clear confirmation screen**, or a **simple weekly streak** can reduce anxiety more effectively than a complex dashboard.

Good UX is not about impressing users — it is about **supporting them**.

If Health Companion helps someone build consistency, gives a caregiver peace of mind, or allows an older adult to feel independent rather than overwhelmed, then the design succeeds.
